Bulls & Bears

4 min read

The term ‚Äúbulls and bears‚Äù has become synonymous with trading thus it's important to understand this metaphor.

In a bull market, the economy is booming and prices are rising. Everyone is having a grand ol' time making a profit. Traders are optimistic about the future - bullish.In other words a bull market is a long-term uptrend.

A bear market is the exact opposite. In a weaker economy where prices fall by at least 20% and the future becomes uncertain, pessimism takes over and traders become bearish.

Bullish outlook is called - "Risk On". It encourages traders to take more risk while the mood is positive.

Bearish outlook is called - "Risk Off".Traders aren't willing to take risky trades in these conditions.

Bear markets are usually shorter in length compared to bull markets.

Of the 18 total bear markets experienced in the last 150 years, the COVID-19 crash in March 2020 saw one of the fastest recoveries.

The US stock market fully recovered in just four months and was back to its pre-crash level by July, soon pushing higher. Due to the way the stock market is set-up, over long-term index prices will almost always rise.

Investing during bear markets?

Unlike traders, investors make money only when prices are rising.

During bear markets they pay close attention to stock market indices like the S&P 500 to gauge the economy. This helps in finding the right moment when prices are at the lowest.
